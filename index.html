What is Agile/Scrum

Scrum is an iterative and incremental agile software development methodology for managing product development.

Roles and actors in Scrum

  • Product Owner
  • Scrum Master
  • Development Team

Elements of Agile/Scrum

  • Backlog
  • Sprint
  • Scrum
  • Daily standup
  • Burndown chart

Product Owner

  • Works to direct the team to the right goal
  • Creating a compelling vision of the product
  • Responsible for prioritizing the backlog during development

Scrum Master

  • Is the team's coach
  • Shelters the team from outside distractions
  • Leads daily stand-ups
  • Focuses on helping the team be the best that it can bet
  • Does not assign tasks to individuals

Scrum Team

  • Each person contributes in whatever way they can to complete the work of each sprint
  • Titles are irellevant within the Scrum team
  • Individuals are expected to work beyond their preferred disciplines whenever doing so would be for the good of the team.
  • Scrum teams manage themselves

Scrum elements

  • Backlog
  • Sprint
  • Daily standup
  • Burndown chart

Backlog

A prioritized features list, containing short descriptions of all functionality desired in the product.

  • Owned by the product owner
  • Consists of user stories

Sprint

Is the basic unit of development

Is a timeboxed effort; it is restricted to a specific duration

  • Should result in a done product
    • Bonus question: How do you define 'done'?
  • Consists of a subset of the backlog
  • Each Sprint starts with a Sprint Planning Event
  • Each Sprint ends with a Sprint Review / Sprint Retrospective

Daily Stand-up/Scrum

Daily short meeting where each member of the development team comes prepared to answer 3 questions

  1. What did I do yesterday that helped the development team meet the sprint goal?
  2. What will I do today to help the development team meet the sprint goal?
  3. Do I see any impediment that prevents me or the development team from meeting the sprint goal?

Important: No detailed discussions take place at the stand-up

Burndown Chart

The sprint burndown chart is a public displayed chart showing remaining work in the sprint backlog

  • Updated daily
  • Sprint burndown, Release Burndown, etc...

Getting started with Agile Scrum

First steps

  1. Find a designated Product Owner
  2. Product Owner to define the product
  3. Build product backlog
  4. Priotize the product backlog

High level estimates

Requirements: A backlog of user stories

  • High level estimate (guestimates)
    • Pro tip: use story points for now, not time estimates
  • Estimate stories relative to each other
    • Pro tip: use Fibonacci sequence, 1,2,3,5,8,13,21 ... 987 ...
  • Estimate as a team (so we can learn from each other)
  • After estimation: Review the priority of stories

Sprint Planning I

Requirements: High level estimates

  • Scrum master: call the Sprint Planning Meeting
  • Must attend: Product Owner, Dev. team, business analysts, testers
  • Meeting agenda
    1. Decide Sprint length
    2. Decide Sprint goal (a done or shippable product)
    3. Clarify Sprint Requirements/spec. the product backlog
    4. Add items from backlog to Sprint Backlog

Sprint Planning II

Requirements: Sprint Planning Meeting I done

  • Primarily for dev. team and product owner
  • Break requirements into tasks
    • Tip: design, development, testing, documentation
    • Pro tip: State tasks as deliverables
  • Estimate tasks in hours as a team
    • Pro tip: Estimate as either 1,2,4 or 8 hours(round up)
  • Ensure sum(tasks hours) <= hours available. Otherwise, adjust by removing items from the Sprint Backlog

Sprint

Requirements: Sprint Planning Meeting II done

  • Primarily for Scrum master and developers. Product Owner welcome.
  • Daily standup, mandatory
  • Scrum master to keep distractions away and remove impediments
  • Product Owner to clarify user stories
  • Update burndown chart daily

After Sprint / Sprint review

Requirements: Sprint is done

  • Participants: Scrum Team, Product Manager, Stakeholders
  • Purpose of Sprint Review:
    • Team to show what has been achieved in the Sprint
    • Stakeholders to see the result and provide feedback
    • Keeps the team focused on the Sprint -> we know we need to demo our achievements

Sprint Retrospective

Requirements: Sprint Review

  • Participants: Scrum Team, Product Manager
  • Internal discussion on how the next Sprints can be done better
  • Elements of the Sprint Retrospective:
    • Review Burndown Chart
    • Discuss what went well in the Sprint
    • Discuss what didn't go well in the Sprint
    • Discuss and decided what changes to make in the next Sprints
      • Pro tip: Pick one or two action items and follow up on next restrospective

Final thoughts

  • Expect 3-5 cycles of Sprints before you start to feel comfortable about it
  • All beginnings are hard, it is a learning process
  • Later: You will begin experimenting within Scrum and adept it to fit you better
